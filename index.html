Ganymede:

Ganymede is the largest natural satellite in the Solar System and is one of Jupiter's 95 known moons. Scientists study Ganymede because it has its own magnetic field and is believed to have a large ocean of salty liquid water hidden beneath its icy surface. Learning about Ganymede helps scientists understand how moons form and whether they could support simple forms of life.

Scientists believe Ganymede formed about 4.5 billion years ago from the cloud of gas, dust, and ice that surrounded Jupiter while the planet was forming. Gravity pulled this material together to create the moon over millions of years. Ganymede is made of rock, metal, and large amounts of water ice. It has a metallic core, a rocky mantle, and a thick icy outer layer that may hide an underground ocean.

Ganymede has a surface made of ice and rock with many craters, bright grooves, ridges, and darker older regions. Scientists think the grooves were formed by movement in the icy crust long ago. Ganymede has a diameter of about 5,268 kilometres, making it the largest moon in the Solar System—even bigger than the planet Mercury. It orbits Jupiter at a distance of about 1.07 million kilometres and takes about 7 Earth days to complete one orbit.
